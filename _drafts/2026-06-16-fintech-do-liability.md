Directors and officers (D&O) insurance protects founders and board members against personal liability arising from lawsuits, regulatory actions, and claims that they failed in their duties managing the company. For most FinTech founders, D&O becomes a priority when investors require it. The term sheet says "obtain D&O coverage at $3 million or $5 million limits," and the policy gets placed under deadline pressure to close the round.

The policy that gets placed is designed to cover shareholder lawsuits and investor disputes. It does that well. What it doesn't do well is cover the risk FinTech founders are most likely to face: regulatory enforcement.

Your D&O policy protects you from your investors' lawyers. It may not protect you from your regulators.

## Where FinTech Founders Are Personally Exposed

FinTech founders carry personal liability across four categories that traditional technology startup founders don't.

In 2025, FinCEN's average enforcement penalty was nearly $13 million. Block Inc. (Cash App) settled for $80 million after investigators found over 169,000 unprocessed suspicious activity report alerts. These are large-company numbers, but the enforcement pattern applies at every scale. FinCEN doesn't have a minimum company size for personal liability.

When Synapse Financial Technologies, a banking-as-a-service middleware company, collapsed in 2024, approximately $265 million in customer funds were frozen because nobody could reconcile which deposits belonged to whom. The regulatory fallout extended to individual officers at partner institutions. FinTechs that custody or intermediate funds face a version of this exposure every day, scaled to their transaction volume.

## Where Standard D&O Falls Short

Standard D&O policies were designed for traditional corporate governance risk: shareholder lawsuits, executive mismanagement claims, securities litigation. They work well for those exposures. FinTech founders face a different risk profile, and three blind spots show up when you read the policy language.

### 1. The Regulatory Exclusion

Many D&O policies contain a "regulatory exclusion" or "government entity exclusion" that limits or eliminates coverage for claims brought by government agencies. Some policies exclude regulatory proceedings entirely. Others cap regulatory defense costs at a sub-limit, often $250,000 or $500,000, against a regulatory action that could cost millions to defend.

I've reviewed D&O policies where the regulatory defense sub-limit was less than 5% of the full policy limit. The founders had no idea.

If FinCEN brings an enforcement action against your company and names you personally, your D&O policy's response depends on one question: does the policy treat a government enforcement action the same as a private lawsuit? Most standard policies do not.

Fines and penalties are almost always excluded entirely. D&O covers defense costs and sometimes settlement payments. It does not cover the fine itself. With the average FinCEN penalty running into eight figures, the fine is the largest component of the loss, and it is entirely uninsured.

### 2. Fiduciary Duty Beyond Shareholders

This is the gap that's hardest to see until you read the policy language next to a balance sheet.

Standard D&O policies define "fiduciary duty" as the duty directors and officers owe to shareholders and to the company. That framing makes sense for a traditional corporation. For a FinTech that holds customer funds in "for benefit of" (FBO) accounts, manages customer investment portfolios, or processes millions in daily transaction volume, the fiduciary exposure doesn't run to shareholders. It runs to customers.

When I read the D&O policy for a payment FinTech holding customer funds in FBO accounts, the "wrongful act" definition pointed at duties owed to the company and its shareholders. The customers whose money sat in those accounts weren't mentioned. If those funds were frozen or misallocated, and customers or a regulator brought a claim alleging the founders failed in their duty to safeguard customer assets, the D&O policy's response would depend on whether "fiduciary duty" in the policy extends beyond shareholders. In most standard forms, it doesn't.

This gap plays out differently depending on what your FinTech does. A payment processor holding funds in transit has custody exposure. A lending platform making credit decisions has fair lending and Equal Credit Opportunity Act (ECOA) liability. A robo-advisor managing portfolios has investment fiduciary duty under the Advisers Act. Each of these creates personal liability for founders that doesn't map to a standard D&O policy written for a software company.

### 3. The Fundraising Freeze

D&O coverage for most FinTechs is frozen at the terms set when the policy was first placed. The policy went in with $3 million in limits for a 15-person pre-revenue startup. Two years later, the company has 60 employees, processes $200 million annually, operates across 15 states, and holds money transmitter licenses in each one.

The D&O limits, the definition of "insured persons," the territory, the regulatory defense provisions: none of these changed because no trigger event forced a review. The funding round closed. The policy renewed on autopilot. I see this constantly. I [wrote about the pattern]({{ '/insights/fintech-identity-crisis/' | relative_url }}) as part of a broader structural problem in FinTech insurance programs. For D&O specifically, the consequences are personal. The limits that were adequate for a seed-stage company aren't adequate for a company with multi-state regulatory exposure and hundreds of millions in transaction volume.

There's a related problem buried in the policy structure. D&O policies have three insuring agreements. The one that matters when the company fails is Side A: it covers individual officers when the company can't indemnify them. Side B and C reimburse the entity. Most FinTech D&O policies are placed with Side B and C in mind, because at the time the policy goes in, the company is healthy. But when a FinTech fails, Side A is the only protection that survives. If Side A shares its limit with entity coverage, founders may find those limits consumed by company defense costs before their individual protection kicks in. The fix is a dedicated Side A limit, or a standalone Side A policy. That's what the Synapse collapse looked like from the insurance side: the company was gone, and individual officers needed Side A coverage that was never separated from the entity tower.

## What D&O for FinTechs Should Cover

If you run a FinTech, here is what your D&O coverage should get right.

<div style="min-width: 32px; height: 32px; background: #00D4AA; border-radius: 50%; display: flex; align-items: center; justify-content: center; font-weight: 700; color: #0A2540; font-size: 16px;">1</div>
<div><strong>Full regulatory defense limits.</strong> Regulatory proceedings should access the full policy limit, not a sub-limit. This is negotiable at renewal. Search your exclusions for "regulatory," "government," or "enforcement" in any limitation or sub-limit.</div>

<div><strong>Broad regulatory definition.</strong> The policy should define "claim" to include administrative and regulatory proceedings, not just civil lawsuits. If your policy only covers "civil actions" or "lawsuits filed in court," FinCEN enforcement falls outside coverage.</div>
</div>
<div style="display: flex; gap: 16px; margin-bottom: 20px;">

<div><strong>Review triggers beyond renewal.</strong> Re-evaluate your D&O at every fundraising round, new state license, new product launch, and new bank partnership. Autopilot renewals are how the Fundraising Freeze happens.</div>

<div><strong>Coordination across your insurance program.</strong> D&O, cyber, and crime policies should work together, not in isolation. ## What "Right" Looks Like

Better products exist. At least one specialty underwriter offers a blended FinTech policy that combines D&O, cyber, crime, and Tech E&O into a single form with one set of definitions. That solves the coordination gap I described in point four above: one carrier, one set of exclusions, no finger-pointing between policies. A few global brokers have built similar bundled programs for FinTech clients.

Most FinTechs have never been shown these products. A generalist broker, or even a startup-focused platform optimized for speed, typically places standard technology policies and doesn't access the specialty markets where the combined forms live. And the right limit isn't a fixed number. It should scale with your regulatory footprint and transaction volume, not just headcount or revenue.
