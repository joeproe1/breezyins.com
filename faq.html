Frequently Asked Questions

Common questions about Risk Intelligence Reports, insurance audits, and working with Breezy.

Is Breezy Risk Advisors an insurance broker?

▼

No. I don't sell insurance, place policies, or earn commissions. Breezy is an independent risk advisory firm. I review and audit your existing insurance program and report what I find. If you want to act on the findings, you take them to your broker, or I can help you find one. But the audit is the product, not a step toward a sale.

What is the Risk Intelligence Report, and how is it different from a quote?

▼

A quote tells you the premium and a high-level description of coverage. It rarely tells you where the real gaps are or how your policies perform against realistic claim scenarios.

The Risk Intelligence Report is a line-by-line analysis that flags where coverage will respond, be disputed, or fail, and what to fix first.

  • Coverage gaps tied to the services you deliver or the risks you face
  • For banks: how cyber, fidelity bond, and D&O interact (and where each carrier points to the other two)
  • For MSPs: contract compliance issues (limits, deductibles, wording, notice requirements)
  • Scenario testing so you can see how claims are likely to be evaluated
  • Prioritized recommendations with fix options
Best fit: Organizations that want to know whether their insurance will hold up in practice.

How is this different from what my broker already does?

▼

Your broker places insurance and earns a commission on what they sell. That is a useful service, but it is not an independent assessment.

I serve a similar function to an external auditor. I read the full policy wording, test it against specific incident scenarios, and report what I find. Most broker reviews focus on limits and premiums. I focus on the policy language that determines whether a claim gets paid.

Many clients use my report to have a more informed conversation with their current broker. Some use it to hold their broker accountable. Either way, my goal is clarity, not replacement.

Do you work with community banks and credit unions?

▼

Yes. Community banks and credit unions are a primary focus. Banks carry three overlapping policies (cyber, fidelity bond, D&O) that interact in ways most brokers review in isolation. I read all three together and map which policy responds to each incident type: wire fraud, ransomware, regulatory investigation, board liability.

The report is built for three audiences: your board (executive brief with dollar ranges), your compliance team or examiner (full analysis with policy-language citations), and your broker (specific findings to renegotiate coverage at renewal).

What's the difference between Tech E&O and cyber liability insurance?

▼

Tech E&O (Errors & Omissions) covers claims alleging financial loss arising from errors or failures in your professional services.

Cyber liability covers cyber incidents like ransomware and data breaches, including incident response costs (forensics, legal, notification) and liability tied to compromised data.

Most MSPs need both. The practical issue is that policies often draw the boundary differently, which can create gray areas for services like incident response support, security assessments, or managed security work.

Do MSPs need different insurance than other tech companies?

▼

Usually, yes. Standard insurance policies often define covered services too narrowly for MSP and security work, such as penetration testing, vulnerability assessments, monitoring, and incident response support.

MSPs also have a distinct third-party exposure because you operate inside client environments. One incident can trigger multiple client claims.

Do I need to switch brokers?

▼

No. The Risk Intelligence Report is a standalone engagement. No broker of record letter required. No commitment to move your insurance.

Most clients use the report to work more effectively with their current broker. The value is knowing exactly what your policies say, where the gaps are, and what to prioritize before your next renewal.

What happens after I receive the report?

▼

You choose how to use it:

  • Take it to your current broker to close the gaps and align coverage.
  • Share it with your board or examiner as documented evidence of your coverage review.
  • Use it as a baseline to measure improvements at your next renewal.

The report includes specific findings and prioritized recommendations. You and your broker decide what to act on first.
